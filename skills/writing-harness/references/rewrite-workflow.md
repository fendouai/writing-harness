# Rewrite Workflow

Use this workflow when the draft needs more than light cleanup.

## Step 1. Define the job

State what the text is supposed to do:

- explain
- persuade
- reassure
- teach
- sell
- frame a decision

If the goal is unclear, infer it from context or ask only if the difference changes the rewrite.

## Step 2. Define the reader

Identify:

- who the reader is
- what they already know
- what they need now
- what they may resist or misunderstand

Writing that addresses "everyone" usually collapses into generic explanation.

## Step 3. Extract the thesis

Write the central claim in one or two lines.

If you cannot state the thesis cleanly, the draft is probably compensating with fluency instead of meaning.

## Step 4. Tag the failure modes

Before rewriting, label the actual problems:

- `generic_abstraction`
- `overexplained`
- `no_real_thesis`
- `false_balance`
- `stock_phrase`
- `weak_example`
- `empty_empathy`
- `uniform_rhythm`
- `insightless_summary`
- `speaker_invisible`

Do not jump straight to word-level cleanup.

## Step 5. Rewrite the logic, then the wording

Fix these in order:

1. thesis strength
2. reader fit
3. specificity
4. structure and emphasis
5. sentence-level language

Do not waste time polishing a weak argument.

## Step 6. Increase specificity

Replace vague nouns and claims with:

- examples
- scenes
- named constraints
- consequences
- tradeoffs
- sharper distinctions

## Step 7. Add judgment

Where the draft tries to sound complete by balancing everything, rewrite toward priority:

- what matters most
- what matters less
- when advice works
- when it breaks

## Step 8. Fix rhythm

Read for repetition:

- same sentence length
- same paragraph shape
- same tone throughout

Break the pattern when emphasis or flow improves.

## Step 9. Score the result

Use `rubric.md`.

If core dimensions remain weak, revise again.
